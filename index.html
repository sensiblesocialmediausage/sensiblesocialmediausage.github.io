What the Research Shows

A paper from 2016 showed that increased time spent on social media was correlated with increased risk of depressive symptoms1. For users that spend who's Daily Social Media Usage (DSMU) was between 61 and 120 minutes, there was an 84% increase in the risk of depression.

In a separate paper from 2017, using more than four social media accounts was associated with a 116% (between 5-6 accounts) and 208% (more than 6 accounts) increase in self-reported depressive symptoms 2. In the same paper, using more than 4 accounts was associated with an 89% (between 5-6) and 227% (more than 6 accounts) increased risk of anxiety.

These risks are further highlighted by the fact that 92% of teens go online every day and a quarter are online "almost constantly". In addition, prominant policy makers have recognized this issue. The American Academy of Pediatricians recognizes “Facebook Depression” as a phenomenon and advises parents to talk to their children and adolescents about online use. The American Medical Assocation in November adopted policy that urges schools to provide safe and effective educational programs through which students can learn to identify and mitigate the onset of mental health impacts caused by social media usage.

Fast Facts
  • 68% of all US adults use Facebook, with 76% of them saying they check it daily (Duggan)
  • 71% of American teenagers use Facebook, 52% use Instagram, 41% use Snapchat, 24% of teens “go online almost constantly”, and 92% go online every day (Lenhart)
  • There are positive and significant correlations between symptoms of anxiety and depressiona dn addcitive use of social media (Andreassen)
  • The use of Facebook is negatively associated with well-being (Kross and Shakya)
  • There is evidence that increased use of social media is linked with higher levels of anxiety and depression in adolescents (Woods)
  • There is evidence that spending more time on Facebook and/or viewing Facebook more frequently, provides people with the opportunity to spontaneously engage in social comparisons, which in turn, is associated with greater depressive symptoms (Steers)
  • The American Psychological Association has found that 48% of millennials (ages 18-37 years old), 37% of Gen Xers (ages 38-51 years old), and 22% of baby boomers (52-70 years old) worry about the negative effects of social media on their physical and mental health (APA)
  • The American Academy of Pediatrics recognizes depression that develops when preteens and teens spend a great deal of time on social media sites, and advises parents to talk to their children and adolescents about their online use (O'Keefe)
  • There are school-based mental health programs that have evidence of positive impact across a range of emotional and behavioral problems, there are no programs that address the association between social media usage and negative mental health sequelae (Rones and Drulak)
