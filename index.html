What the Research Shows

A paper from 2016 showed that increased time spent on social media was correlated with increased risk of depressive symptoms1. For users that spend who's Daily Social Media Usage (DSMU) was between 61 and 120 minutes, there was an 84% increase in the risk of depression.

In a separate paper from 2017, using more than four social media accounts was associated with a 116% (between 5-6 accounts) and 208% (more than 6 accounts) increase in self-reported depressive symptoms 2. In the same paper, using more than 4 accounts was associated with an 89% (between 5-6) and 227% (more than 6 accounts) increased risk of anxiety.

These risks are further highlighted by the fact that 92% of teens go online every day and a quarter are online "almost constantly". In addition, prominant policy makers have recognized this issue. The American Academy of Pediatricians recognizes “Facebook Depression” as a phenomenon and advises parents to talk to their children and adolescents about online use. The American Medical Assocation in November adopted policy that urges schools to provide safe and effective educational programs through which students can learn to identify and mitigate the onset of mental health impacts caused by social media usage.
